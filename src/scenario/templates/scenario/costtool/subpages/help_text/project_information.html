Design Elements: This section summarizes the level of compliance that is met by the Project. Developers and designers need to consider stormwater management requirements for a Project site. There are three elements of stormwater compliance requirements that a developer must address:

  1. Nutrient management
  2. Stormwater volume requirements
  3. Peak flow mitigation

Level 1 - Nutrient Management

According to the City of Raleigh's Unified Development Ordinance (UDO) Section 9.2.2.B.1. Nitrogen Reduction:

C. Stormwater control measures shown on the stormwater control master plan for a new development or expansion of existing development that are demonstrated to control stormwater runoff on a runoff volume basis will be deemed to meet the nitrogen export load requirement, provided that the post-development volume of stormwater leaving the site is equal to or less than the pre-development volume, based on the 90th percentile storm. The pre- development land cover must be assumed to be forested for the entire site.

A developer that does not meet nutrient load requirements has the option to "buy down" a portion of their nutrient load from a DWR approved nutrient offset provider to meet loading rate targets . The buy down requirements are different across watersheds and should be considered for the Cost Analysis.

Level 2 - Stormwater Volume Requirements

According to the City of Raleigh's UDO Section 9.5.2.C. 3. & 9.5.3.C.3.a.:

[Note: Four criteria listed for exception approval] In requesting a design exception, the developer must demonstrate that runoff resulting from the first inch of rainfall over the entire development site will be detained or retained using conventional stormwater treatment practices, GSI, or a combination.

Stormwater practices that are designed to manage nutrient mitigation (see Level 1) must also be designed to detain both the water quality one-inch storm and the one year, 24-hour three-inch storm.

Level 3 - Peak Flow Mitigation

According to the City of Raleigh's UDO Section 9.2.2.E Stormwater Runoff Controls:

The peak stormwater runoff leaving any site for the two-year and 10-year storms shall be no greater at every point of discharge for post-development conditions than pre-development conditions. The same methodologies used to calculate stormwater runoff must be used for both pre-development and post-development conditions...

Developers need to consider peak flow control on their sites, which can be established using conventional (Gray Infrastructure) end-of-pipe designs and non-conventional distributed (Green Infrastructure) structures. Sites that do not meet peak flow requirements will need to be reevaluated and undergo changes in design so that peak flow requirements are met.

The user should also be aware about the opportunities of savings made available when certain compliance requirements are met:

Green Raleigh Review

The City has added a new program called the Green Raleigh Review, which is supposed to incentivize developers to include more GSI in their design. Participants in this program will get reimbursements for two fees:

  1. Sketch Plan Review Fee
  2. Express Service Fee

This program requires participants to show that GSI practices are in their design plans and that the design mitigates stormwater runoff volume to the pre-project 90th percentile storm event conditions. This is verified using the Storm EZ Permitting Tool. If the project plans are approved, the participant is eligible for review fee reimbursements.

Pervious/Impervious: This section summarizes the amount of land in the Scenario of the Project site that is expected to be pervious (porous area where water can infiltrate) or impervious (non-porous area where water cannot infiltrate). These values are important when comparing Scenarios within a Project site because developers are subject to stormwater fees based on the amount of impervious surface the Project site has. GSI can mitigate the amount of impervious surface is on site by replacing surfaces of different landscape features like asphalt and rooftop with porous pavers or vegetation, respectively.

The single-family rate consists of five tiers that are based on the amount of impervious surface (in square feet) of a property:

  • Tier One-400 to 1000 square feet = $2.00/month
  • Tier Two-1,001 to 3,870 square feet = $5.00/month
  • Tier Three-3,871 to 6,620 square feet = $8.50/month
  • Tier Four-6,621 to 9,500 square feet = $14.50/month
  • Tier Five-Over 9,500 square feet is billed at the commercial rate = Total Impervious surfaces divided by 2,260 (1 SFEU) times $5 = monthly fee

The Commercial rate (e.g. multifamily, office, institutional, commercial industrial land uses; including single family parcels over 9,500 square feet)

  • $5 per 2,260 square feet of impervious surfaces
  • A commercial fee is calculated by the following formula. Total Impervious surfaces divided by 2,260 (1 SFEU) times $5 = monthly fee

One Single-Family Equivalent Unit (SFEU) represents the median impervious surface area found on a single-family property.

Note: properties with less than 400 square feet of impervious surfaces are not subject to a Stormwater fee.

Areas exclusively designated to stormwater management

Areas used exclusively for treating stormwater, generally Structural Best Management Practices (BMPs) or Stormwater Control Measures (SCMs), are designed to manage large volumes of stormwater for complying with regulations for reducing stormwater peak flow rates, stormwater volume, and/or pollutant concentrations, depending on regulatory requirements and performance objectives. Gray Infrastructure SCMs can require permanent dedication of large land areas (e.g., wet ponds, dry ponds, stormwater wetlands) and usually are located along the downslope periphery of project sites. Green Infrastructure stormwater management practices, when distributed within other site features, can reduce the land area needed for treating stormwater and make more land area available for other uses and/or reduce the land area needed for the development, possibly resulting in cost savings or added value for the development.

Potential for savings:
  • Value of freed up land per square foot ($/ft2)
Amenity Areas / Urban Plaza

Many development sites are required by the City's UDO to have an amenity area or an urban plaza. An amenity area is any feature that provides comfort, convenience, or pleasure. An urban plaza can be a public square or an open area near urban buildings featuring walkways and places to sit that are surrounded by trees and shrubs. The UDO prescribes size requirements for amenity areas and urban plazas based on the type of development and the development site. Amenity areas and urban plazas usually contribute to the amount of impervious surface area on a development site and thereby contribute to the site's stormwater treatment requirements. However, the City's UDO allows placement of GSI practices within amenity areas and urban plazas, which can reduce the extent of stormwater treatment to be provided elsewhere on the site. These sections of the UDO address placing GSI practices within amenity areas or urban plazas:

UDO Section 1.5.3.C.7. Stormwater detention wet ponds and dry ponds shall not be considered an outdoor amenity area. GSI practices may be used to meet up to 10% of the requirement for the amenity area.

UDO Section 1.5.3.D.2. (For Urban Plazas) In addition, vegetated GSI practices may be located within the amenity area and may be used to meet up to 10% of the requirement for the amenity area. Stormwater detention wet ponds and dry ponds shall not be considered an outdoor amenity area.

GSI practices that may be placed within amenity areas and urban plazas include:
  • Swales,
  • Bioretention,
  • Filter Strips,
  • Planter Boxes, and
  • Permeable Pavement
Potential for savings:
  • Reduction in stormwater utility fees via reduction in impervious surface area ($/ft2/year)
  • Reduction in City review fees via Green Raleigh Review ($)
  • Reduction in land area needed exclusively for treating stormwater ($)
  • Value of freed-up land per square foot ($/ft2)

Protective Yards

Protective yards are landscaping features used to obstruct the view of a development site from an adjacent property or to otherwise serve as a visual buffer between disparate land uses. The City's UDO allows placement of GSI practices within protective yards, which can reduce the extent of stormwater treatment to be provided elsewhere on the site. These sections of the UDO address placing GSI practices within protective yards:

UDO Section 7.2.4.A & 7.2.4.B GSI practices shall be allowed in Transitional Protective Yard Types A2, B1, and B2 and in Street Protective Yard Types C1, C2, and C3. In order to accommodate GSI practices the number of shrubs may be reduced in the Protective Yards by ten (10) percent.

UDO Section 7.2.7.C.4 Additional Requirements for Trees in a Protective Yards a. In a protective yard, 50% of required trees shall be locally-adaptive evergreen species, except where an approved GSI practice is within a protective yard.

5.b.vi. Shrubs, Protective Yard To accommodate multi-functional GSI practices as part of an approved stormwater management plan, the number of shrubs may be reduced in the Protective Yards by 10%, non-evergreen species may be used in lieu of up to 35% of evergreen shrubs, and all shrubs may be 24 inches when planted.

GSI practices that may be placed within protective yards include:
  • Swales,
  • Bioretention, and
  • Filter Strips
Potential for savings:
  • Reduction in City review fees via Green Raleigh Review ($)
  • Reduction in land area needed exclusively for treating stormwater ($)
  • Value of freed-up land per square foot ($/ft2)

Building

On many development sites buildings contribute a substantial portion of the site's total impervious area and thereby a substantial portion of the site's requirement for stormwater treatment. Designs for development buildings can provide for rooftop materials or other systems for managing building rooftop runoff that can reduce the extent of stormwater treatment to be provided elsewhere on the site. Green roofs also can reduce the impervious surface area of a site.

Drive-thru Facility

A drive-thru facility is feature that designates space within the Project site for the provision or dispersion of products and/or services from a business or automated machine. These features typically require a compact evergreen hedge to screen the vehicles passing through this part of the site. However, updates to Raleigh's UDO allow for GSI practices in this setting.

UDO Section 7.2.5.A.4 (Drive Thru Facilities) The following options may be used in lieu of compact evergreen hedge (1) a combination of plants within GSI practices proposed to be part of an approved management plan and evergreen plants outside such GSI practices that together, at the time of planting, provide screening at least 36 inches above the level of the ground adjacent to the GSI practice, and reach a height of at least 48 inches above adjacent ground within 3 years of planting.

The updates allow GSI to be a standard component of drive-thru facilities to help with onsite stormwater management. GSI that can occur in drive-thru facilities include:

  • Bioretention, and
  • Planter Boxes.

Potential for savings:

  • Reduction in Green Raleigh Review fees ($)
  • Reduction in land needed exclusively for stormwater management ($)

Miscellaneous Landscaping / Open Space (excluding vegetated stormwater practices)

Miscellaneous landscaping and open space are features within a Project site that are included to provide aesthetics to the site. This is additional land that is not part of the legally required space, but is just open space in the Project site. These can have a variety of plants and species present, and can be a place for recreation or leisure in the site. These spaces may be left in its natural state and become managed open space, can include conventional structures like lawns, shrubs, and tree plantings, or can be areas where GSI is incorporated to increase overall stormwater management and aesthetics to the property.

GSI that can occur in miscellaneous landscaping and open space include:

  • Bioretention cells

Streets

Streets have several development requirements that can impact a site's overall stormwater runoff. For this feature, developers need to consider vegetative requirements within required planting areas and size requirements of the road width. Changes in the UDO allows developers to consider the use of GSI in planting areas while maintaining understory tree requirements to better mitigate stormwater.

UDO Section 8.4.1.D & 8.5.1.D. New Street and Existing Street Tree Planting

  1. Where overhead utilities exist, 1 understory tree shall be planted every 20 feet on center, on average. Required understory trees may be offset by installing vegetated GSI practices, such as stormwater planter boxes. A maintenance plan must be approved for the GSI practice.
  2. All required street trees must meet the design and installation requirements of Sec.7.2.7. If a GSI practice is part of an approved stormwater management plan for the site, required street trees may be installed within the GSI practice. A maintenance plan must be approved for the GSI practice.

UDO Section 8.5.2. The required streetscape type is determined by the zoning district or by the designated frontage Dimensional standards for planting area, tree spacing, and utility placement and the planting types may be varied to accommodate GSI practices. The Planning and Development Officer and the Urban Forester shall make the final determination.

The updates allow GSI to be a standard component of this feature to help with onsite stormwater management.

GSI that can occur in streets include:
  • Planter Boxes
